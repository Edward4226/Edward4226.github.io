> The diploma as a credit instrument, and the guarantors all failing to back it at once.

## 0. The step I didn't see coming, three years ago

In June 2023 I wrote down a hunch: the direction of technological progress is to make production need fewer people. But back then my picture of "technology" stopped at tools — and a tool replaces hands and feet. However advanced it gets, the thinking is still left to a person. What I couldn't imagine was this: intelligence itself could be produced, priced, and sold by the unit.

Three years on, the commoditization of intelligence is real. Its earliest trace showed up in a dataset almost nobody reads closely.

The largest payroll processor in the United States is called ADP; every month it cuts paychecks for tens of millions of people. The ledger in its hands is earlier and finer-grained than any government statistic — a first-hand set of books. In 2025, someone went digging and found something strange: in the jobs most exposed to AI — software development, customer support — employment for 22-to-25-year-olds dropped a notch, roughly 13% to 16%, increasingly clear from 2024 on. Meanwhile senior staff in the very same roles didn't move. Jobs AI can't yet reach were fine across every age bracket.

Note: the companies didn't fire anyone. They just quietly stopped hiring new people. The seats are still there; they're just no longer filled.

The person who wrote that report called it "Canaries in the Coal Mine." Miners used to carry a canary down the shaft; the moment gas leaked in, the bird went down before the people did — a living alarm. Let me put it more plainly: the bird that fell first is the fresh graduate. And the mine, slowly losing its air, is the entire "study → diploma → good job" exchange system.

From here on I'm not going to wax lyrical. I'm going to run an audit.

## 1. A diploma is a credit note

Let's set the definition straight: a diploma certainly has value, but its value comes in two layers, wildly different in size.

One layer is capability value — you did learn something, and that part is real; more on it later. The other is credit value: the market sees the slip of paper, assumes by default that you're smart enough, can sit still long enough, follow rules well enough, and is willing to price you at "face value." Of these two, credit is the larger share. A single comparison makes the point: one more year of schooling raises an individual's income by about ten percent, but one more year of average schooling raises a whole country's GDP by only two or three percent. The individual return is more than four times the social return. If a diploma's worth came mainly from real learning, those two numbers shouldn't differ fourfold — the gap is credit at work, not knowledge.

So the truest analogy for a diploma isn't a commodity, it's a banknote. The interesting thing about a modern banknote is that it promises you nothing — nobody guarantees how much rice a hundred yuan will buy. It circulates because of the issuer's credit, and because the largest party accepting it keeps accepting it. A diploma is exactly the same: no clause anywhere says "bachelor's degree = a salary above ten thousand a month." What this note can be exchanged for depends entirely on the price the whole credit system is willing to accept it at.

So who holds this system up? I count four roles: the university that issues the note, the big-company hiring desk that redeems it, the social consensus that circulates it at face value, and — pressed underneath the whole thing — the collateral: your actual skills.

I'll put the conclusion up front: all four are failing at once.

## 2. The redeemer: closing the acceptance window

First, the redeemer. A diploma can be exchanged for a good job because someone is willing to accept the note with a good job — for the past several decades, mainly the big-company hiring desk.

What's happening on that side, I half-said in the opening: big companies aren't firing, they're declining to hire newcomers. Three more details. Within the same company, demand for junior roles has dropped by roughly a third while demand for senior roles has barely moved; this is happening almost only in tech, not elsewhere; and the entry-level seats that "ought" to be the safest, most protected by ordinary logic, are the ones falling hardest.

Pull any one of these three out and you can find another explanation; put them together and only one explanation holds. Even the economists who first spotted the canary say AI isn't the only variable — but the only thing that strings all three together into one line is AI.

Why now, of all times? Because the hiring desk's arithmetic changed for the first time. However advanced machines got in the past, what they replaced was hands and feet; "entry-level cognitive work" as a commodity had exactly one supplier: the graduate. Not anymore. AI is the first "graduate" in history that needs no diploma, no benefits package, and bills by the unit — and what it sells is the very thing graduates sell: entry-level cognitive labor. Run the procurement math: a family spends a small fortune and a dozen-plus years raising one degree-holder who then waits for a monthly salary; on the company's side, you subscribe to an intelligence for a few hundred a month, and the price for equivalent capability falls by roughly an order of magnitude a year. Two quotes, on the same desk, for the first time. The new internal rule at some Silicon Valley firms — prove the AI can't do this job before you request a new headcount — translates to: the hiring budget and the API budget have started substituting for each other. The university's competitor is no longer another university; it's the pricing department at an AI company.

So look at those two curves again: per-head revenue at these companies is hitting record highs, while headcount drifts down. Money earned and people employed — two lines that used to rise together — have forked. The diploma is pinned to the one that's heading down. The acceptance window isn't shut, but the opening is narrowing.

## 3. The issuer: a machine that buys peace with time

The redeemer is tightening. If the issuer would show some restraint, the note might still hold. But the issuer is doing the opposite — and what it's doing runs deeper than "printing too much."

Start with the over-printing itself. A diploma's worth rests, like gold's, on scarcity: few people have it, so it's dear. Yet this machine has spent two decades destroying that scarcity with its own hands. Take the number of graduates each year and divide it by the population born in the same cohort: within a single generation, the share who end up holding a bachelor's degree rose from about 3.5% in the late 1990s to about 28% today — a seven-to-eightfold jump. Gold is dear because there's only so much of it on Earth; this particular "gold," the issuer mines a fresh seam of out of thin air every year.

But the issuer prints frantically not out of greed — out of procrastination.

Think about what the expansion actually "created." It conjured no jobs out of thin air. What it really did was hold one generation of working-age labor back for four more years before tossing it into the job market — graduate school holds them three years longer; a doctorate, longer still. When the crowd is piled up at the door of the labor market and there aren't enough places inside, parking them in school a while longer is the cheapest buffer of all. The university here is less a factory than a reservoir: it manufactures no new work, it merely bets that "in four years there'll be a way." Put bluntly, it's pass-the-parcel — handing today's problem to your four-years-later self, and to the people further down the line.

A fair word here: not every one of those years is water. Some skills — a doctor's, an engineer's — really are stacked up layer by layer over time, and four years, seven years, is not too long. But for far more majors, the genuinely valuable core is taught in the first year or two; the years after that aren't growing real skill so much as padding out the reservoir's dwell time — stretching something that needn't take so long into a four-to-seven-year marathon.

And this reservoir of a machine has a flaw at its root: its clock no longer matches the times.

It's a machine on a fixed four-year cycle. An industry opens up a gap, the signal reaches the universities, they expand enrollment and faculty, then students enroll, finish, graduate — that chain is locked at four years minimum, and won't compress. Translated: where the class of 2026 ends up was, to a large degree, decided back around 2022 by whichever industry was hot then. At eighteen, filling out your application, what you're really doing isn't "planning a life" — it's buying a futures contract on the job market that settles in four years. You place the bet today; it only clears the day you graduate four years later; and at the moment of betting, you know nothing about that industry four years out.

Plainly, choosing a major is gambling. It didn't look like gambling in the past because industries shifted slowly — a four-year lag could still keep pace with a ten- or fifteen-year run of an industry's fortunes. What AI did was speed the market's clock up and crank the volatility wide open. When the rise and fall of an industry is compressed shorter than the time it takes to finish a degree, a four-year-cycle machine cannot, structurally, avoid mistiming. Every school is the same — it isn't a question of how well the school is run, it's that the clock doesn't match.

And the stake on this bet is your whole life. How much a person earns over a lifetime is, for the most part, set in the first ten years of work — yet which year's market you enter on is precisely the thing you can't bet on. Two people, equally smart and equally hardworking, graduating two or three years apart, get pulled a notch apart on starting salary — and that gap doesn't get erased when the economy recovers: fifteen years later, it's still biting into your payslip. Whoever got lucky and rode the top of the market may close the wound in ten years; whoever drew the bottom may carry it for life — unless you can step out of this market that settles by your year of entry, and price yourself. (That way out, I'll save for the end.)

Only now can I say what this section is really about.

Higher education was, from the start, a sieve: a series of stiff exams that screen out, from the crowd, the few who can create extra value for society. It was meant to be unfair — the entire point of a sieve is to screen most people out. The reason a diploma could be pegged to high income was built, on day one, on exactly this: because most can't get through, those who can are rare, and dear.

But over the past few decades, the world quietly remade this sieve into a welfare-and-equality project: subsidize schooling, lower the bar, expand enrollment in bulk, with the goal becoming "everyone gets to go to university." The intent was kind; the effect was self-destructive — widen the mesh until anyone can pass. And a sieve everyone passes is no longer a sieve. The day everyone has a diploma, the diploma proves nothing, and the peg between it and income was quietly snapped at the moment "universalization" was achieved.

This isn't a forecast, it's a definition: once scarcity is destroyed by supply, value collapses on the spot. Gold works this way, and so does the diploma.

## 4. The secondary market: a market with no quote, that falls in silence

Someone will say: so what if the issuer over-prints and the redeemer tightens — as long as everyone still accepts the note at face value, it's still worth something.

Right. That's the third guarantor: consensus. Parents treat admission to a famous school as their most important investment; companies use "first-degree prestige" to screen people; the dating market uses it as a price tag. After the first two guarantors wobbled, it's this layer of recognition still holding the face value up.

The trouble with consensus is that it's always the last to react. Because to admit this note has depreciated is to admit that the road you walked, the road you paved for your kids, all has to be re-priced — and nobody wants to be the first to admit it. So you get a split screen: the canary in the data has long since fallen, and crowds are still lined up to buy in. Parents still urging their kids to "sell the pots and pans but get into grad school," HR still treating a famous school as the only standard — they aren't bad people, they're just people who haven't reset their clocks.

What's interesting is that this time, the recognition is loosening faster than usual, and it's voting with its feet. Lay two sets of application figures side by side: graduate-school applications fell from a peak of 4.74 million in 2023 for three straight years, down to 3.43 million in 2026; over the same window, civil-service exam applications hit record highs year after year, climbing to 3.51 million in 2026.

| Applications (millions) | Grad school | Civil service |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 2023 | 4.74 | 2.50 |
| 2024 | 4.38 | 2.91 |
| 2025 | 3.88 | 3.25 |
| 2026 | 3.43 | 3.51 |
| 3-yr change | −28% | +40% |

One curve sliding down, one climbing up. These two waves needn't be the same people, but the direction says it all: fewer keep leveraging up to buy more credentials, and more head straight for an iron rice bowl. There's a role in financial markets called the market-maker of last resort — when everyone refuses to take an asset, the only counter still posting bids at the old price is the official one. 3.51 million people crowding the civil-service exam is, in essence, a stampede toward the maker of last resort. And the day a note has only the state left willing to take it, it has effectively withdrawn from market circulation.

They may not be able to read ADP's payroll ledger, but the move their feet are making matches the verdict that canary gave, exactly.

## 5. The collateral: even the real skill underneath is losing its premium

The last guarantor is also the optimist's final way out: the note can depreciate, fine — I hold real skill, the collateral is solid.

I have to say this precisely, to avoid being misread. Skill itself is always useful — knowing how to write code, how to analyze, how to communicate is good for you, the person, at any time. What I'm saying is not that skill is useless. It's that the "scarcity premium" the market is willing to pay extra for that skill is going to zero.

The logic is the same as the last section. A skill is worth something not only because it's useful, but because few people have it. And what the commoditization of intelligence does is precisely to erase that "few." If the expansion poured water into the gold mine of the diploma, AI does something harsher to real skill — it strikes the seam right through. Knowing how to do things stops being scarce; the supply of "can do it" surges overnight, and with the scarcity gone, the premium goes too.

A customer-support experiment laid it bare: after the AI assistant was switched on, novices' efficiency rose by about thirty percent, while the most senior veterans barely improved. Level the two ends, and the decade-long lead a veteran built gets wiped in one stroke. AI doesn't eliminate "doing things"; it eliminates the price difference between the people who do them — and the price difference is exactly what you were buying with your years of study, internships, and time put in.

This isn't hypothetical; the freelance market has already tasted it. On the platforms that outsource work to individuals, the incomes of veteran writers and designers are sliding, and the higher the price and the more seasoned the hand, the harder the fall. In every previous wave of automation, high skill was the talisman. This time, for the first time, it's the target.

So all four guarantors are accounted for: the redeemer closing the window, the issuer over-printing and stalling, the consensus loosening, and even the collateral underneath losing value. This note is being pressed down by four hands at once.

## 6. So "study is useless" got half of it right

Put the four guarantors together and the conclusion is cold and clear.

A diploma has always been a note that circulates on credit. Today its issuer, its redeemer, its market of circulation, and its collateral are all failing at once. No one is doing wrong; there's no smoky back room, no villain. Each role is just being shoved along by the same shift, too busy saving itself to have the means — or the motive — to keep this note at its old price.

And that line, scorned for years as anti-intellectual — "study is useless"?

It got half of it right; the people shouting it just can't say which half. The wrong half is "knowledge is useless" — knowledge is always useful to a person; the collateral is real. The right half is: this note no longer holds its value. Ordinary people can't read the data or compute what fraction is credit, but the gut feeling is honest. Getting out before the credit collapses is the most rational thing a noteholder can do. The bird that leaves before the gas rises isn't wrong.

Of course, not every bird in the mine dies. The people who don't rely on someone else stamping their note — who can price themselves under the new rules — were never in the shaft. As for what, exactly, a person should rely on to prove their worth in a world where the note has failed — that's another, larger question, and I'll leave it here for now.

The one thing I want to make clear: the note in your hand is being pressed down by four hands at once. See it for what it is a little earlier, and you won't be the last one still standing in line to buy in.

**Postscript.** The first draft of this was written in June 2023, and it didn't start with diplomas. Back then I was watching something bigger: every step technology takes squeezes a few more people out of production. What I could see then was only software, Excel, machines that need less and less minding — I had a vague sense the line would eventually reach universities and diplomas, but I could never put it cleanly, because I was missing the last piece: I couldn't yet imagine that "thinking" itself could be mass-produced. Three years later, that piece grew in on its own. Its name is AI. So this isn't a piece chasing a trend — it's a judgment set down three years ago that was missing one variable, and the variable has now arrived.
